****KEY IDEA:  It is easier to change your mind about your topic at this stage, than later in your research.  Be certain that you are able to "live with" this topic and that there is enough information about the topic to lead you to suggest a thesis question.

Lincoln's 2nd Inaugural Address

Assignment due on Monday, April 29.

                The period from April, 1865 until after the inauguration of Rutherford B. Hayes in 1877 is known as the Reconstruction Period of American history.   As we know and as Lincoln predicted, there needed to be a “new birth of freedom” (Gettysburg Address,) when the war ended. 

                Be aware that since 1863, Lincoln had been preparing to fight the Congressional Radical Republican wing of his political party which called for an “unconditional surrender “of the Confederacy that would go hand in with a very harsh, vengeful reconstruction.  To distance himself from this radical way of thinking, Lincoln refused to use the term “reconstruction”, preferring the milder “restoration.”

Proposed in 1863, by two Senators, Benjamin Wade of Ohio and Henry Davis of Maryland, the Wade-Davis plan for readmission of the Southern states to the Union would set stringent requirements.  Each conquered state would have a provisional governor, appointed by the president.  When a majority of the white males of the state pledged their allegiance to the Union, the provisional governor could call a state constitutional convention with delegates elected by those white males who would swear that they had never fought against the USA.  These delegates would write a new state constitution that had to include:  abolishment of slavery, disenfranchisement of former Confederate civil and military leaders, and repudiation of any debts accumulated by the state during the War.  Political rights for blacks would be left up to each state.  Only then would Congress readmit a state to the Union.  This bill was passed by both houses of Congress in July, 1864—four months before the Presidential and Congressional elections.  Lincoln promptly pocket vetoed this bill.

  Lincoln was appalled at the attitudes and actions of the Radical Republicans.   Believing that if there was to be true reconciliation of the two sides, Lincoln’s reconciliation plan was called the Ten Percent Solution.  It would require that 10% of those who were eligible to vote in 1860 must take a loyalty oath to the USA.  Lincoln’s plan also offered amnesty to former Confederate soldiers—other than the highest officials—provided that they took a loyalty oath also.  Once these 10% conditions were met, the state could set up a new constitution and government.  Lincoln also hoped that black males who were educated, owned property and had served in the Union Army would be given the right to vote.  By the time of the 1864 Presidential election, four states—Louisiana, Tennessee, and Arkansas—had been readmitted to the Union under Lincoln’s plan. 

The election of 1864 was messy.  Lincoln ran against McClellan—the Union General that he had fired.  Congressional Radical Republicans would not count the electoral votes of the three “newly reconstructed” states.  However, Lincoln was able to get a greater percentage of the popular vote in this election than he had in 1860.  When he stepped to the podium for his inauguration on March 4, 1865, the newly elected Congress was already trying to find ways to confront the Commander-in-Chief, even as the Civil War was winding down.  Of course, Lincoln had no way of knowing that Lee would surrender to Grant April 9, 1864 and that five days later, he would be assassinated.

Due May 08, 2013: Main ideas: American Experience--the Second Civil War

The American Experience:  The Second Civil War

Main ideas:

·         Lincoln’s death and President Johnson’s plans for at Reconstruction create backlash in the victorious Union, the defeated Confederacy, states’ legislatures and Congress.  What has this bloody, destructive, and disturbing war accomplished? 

·         Former slaves and freemen are comforted by the Emancipation Proclamation and the 13th Amendment but discover that it takes more than laws to change the ways in which societies and people believe and behave.

·         Freed slaves had been promised “40 acres and a mule” by the Union military leaders. Some black leaders—often ministers or teachers—organized black-only communities, providing residents with schools, jobs, economic and political support.    However, these black leaders often were targeted (culling the alphas) by white supremacist groups such as the Ku Klux Klan which used intimidation, lynching, and scare tactics such as burning black schools to force blacks’ social submission .

·         The federally funded and developed Freedman’s Bureau helps many former slaves to reunite their families, set up schools, find jobs and reorder their lives.  However, the FB also offers opportunities for unscrupulous Yankees and Southern whites (carpetbaggers and scalawags) to make fortunes off of the misfortunes of unsophisticated, naïve and desperate people, both black and white.

·         Most Southern whites were surprised that former slaves did not want to continue their subservient social position.  Supported by President Johnson’s views against racial equality, these Southerners refused to accept the notion of racial equality.  Utmost in most white Southerners’ minds (wealthy, middle class and poor) was a return to Southerners civility, the concept of honor and moral/religious values.

·         Before Congress passed its Military Reconstruction Act in 1866, many former Confederate states had passed Black Codes.  These laws were designed to keep blacks (former slaves and freemen) “in their place”.  From restricting blacks “freedom of assembly” (even in churches!) to economic rules such as tenant farming/sharecropping and crop liens, blacks’ economic progress in the Southern states was restricted severely.

·         Northern factory owners, bankers and business leaders were anxious to restart the cotton, tobacco and sugar economy, which supported American textile mills, factories, railroads, shipping, international trade, western expansion, etc.  They encouraged former slaves to return to the cotton fields as hired contract laborers.

·         A huge jump in immigrants from Western and Southern Europe increased competition for jobs and land in the North and West for blacks.   This also created race-based discrimination in the North and West, as well as the South.

·         Race riots in the south—New Orleans, Memphis, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia—became commonplace.  The riots in 1866 in New Orleans had to be put down by Federal troops, further angering the former rebels. 

·         The 14th Amendment to the Constitution guarantees all citizens equal rights before the law.  The Southern states complain that this should be a state decision—states’ rights vs. federal rights all over again.  The 15th Amendment that grants black males the right to vote is vehemently challenged also.

·         Radical Republicans realize that the “serpent of white power in the South” must be beheaded.  Therefore, Congress passes the Military Reconstruction Act of 1867 which divides the rebel states into five military districts.  The United States military will occupy these states until new state legislatures have been elected, the 13th, 14th and 15th amendments are passed by the state legislatures, new representation to Congress and state legislatures have been elected.

·         Slowly, the Southern states follow the Reconstruction Act; they are an “occupied territory.”  Black voters elect black representatives to Congress and to state legislatures.  Most of these new participants in freedom are conscientious and serious; some are taken advantage of by unscrupulous whites or blacks who see a chance to “cash in.”

·         The myth of the rapacious black man who lusts after white women (remember To Kill a Mockingbird?) takes root in southern consciousness as black soldiers (and whites) patrol the South.  Popular fiction, political cartoons emphasize the “purity” of the South and the necessity of white males to protect and defend this from outsiders.

·         A severe economic recession of 1873, refocuses Northern business interests and leaders on the national economy; they had grown tired of ruling the South.

·         Events such as the Coushatta Massacre in 1874 demonstrate the depth of some Yankee whites’ venality, the failures of Military Reconstruction, the power of paramilitary hate groups such as the White League, the inability or disinterest of the federal government in protecting black citizens, the political and social power of white supremacists in the Dixiecrat Democratic Party at both the local and national levels.

·         The end of Grant’s second term saw the rise of Redemption thinking across the United States.  Southern gentility, codes of honor, social civility, the importance of religious truths were elevated, whitewashing (pun intended) negative aspects of the southern way of life.   Perhaps God never intended the races to be equal if that meant losing other critical values.  No one asked, “if a person can be discriminated against on the basis of race, has slavery really ended?”

·         By the time of the 1876 Presidential election between Hayes (R) and Tilden (D), national political and social leaders had turned their attentions to other problems—immigration from Eastern Europe, growing poverty and social problems in cities, final subjugation of Indians, international policy, industrial growth.  Hayes had won 165 electoral votes; Tilden had 184.  Twenty electoral votes (Florida, Louisiana, South Carolina, Oregon) were in dispute.  Tilden had scored 247,448 more popular votes than Hayes.  According to the Constitution, the election had to be determined in the House of Representatives.  A bi-partisan, fifteen member Electoral Commission investigated the disputed electoral votes and awarded them to Hayes, giving him the election.  Some historians say that a deal was made between Southerners and Northerners that if Hayes won, he would end Reconstruction. 

·          Reconstruction is over.  Has the Union been Restored?  Is there reconciliation?

Questions for Industrialization Role Play

1.        What should the ideal economic structure look like?

 

2.       What is the best way to ensure prosperity?  (Prosperity for all citizens?)

 

 

3.       At what pace should economic changes be implemented?

 

4.       Should we ever slow down growth if it is producing undesirable social, economic or political effects (or affects?)

 

 

5.       Should there be a minimum wage for workers?  For all workers or only workers in certain fields or with certain skills and/or training?

 

6.       Should industries look after their workers?  In what ways, if any?

9.       Does the economic system affect the political system?
