Rony Cohen: Hebrew LitHonSem

Hebrew Lit (H) Seminar 11/12

This is an advanced course in Hebrew Literature. All classes are conducted completely in Hebrew. The overarching goal is reading, comprehending and valuing Hebrew Literature.

The text we study is an anthology for High School Israeli Literature students. The anthology includes short stories, essays and poems by well known Hebrew writers and poets.

The anthology is arranged by topics and genres. It places the literary pieces, short stories and poetry, in context of the period they were written and makes connections to other writers, in Hebrew or world literature. We analyze the pieces using literary terminology and technique, to determine what is said and how it is said. We discuss ideas and themes brought about by our reading. We compare similar themes presented by different writers in different time periods, or different pieces by the same writer. Students will be able to read and analyze independently similar stories and poems and present interpretations orally and in writing. Students will present thesis papers and creative writing assignments periodically.

Some students participate in writing and editing of our schools Hebrew language student publication "M'et L'et" מעת לעת, a literary magazine that is published three times per year.

Our students use Hebrew word processing and are required to hand in assignments and book reports using computer and a Hebrew language software. Other computer applications and multi-media presentations are introduced to enhance the learning material and encourage students to use appropriate technology (word searches, sources, biographies, enrichments and Hebrew Power Point presentations).

I wish all my students a year of exciting, fulfilling and fun learning experience.

Rony Cohen

Hebrew Department Leader

Overview

May 31st, 2010

by Ray Leven

Hebrew is not only the language of Israel and many Jews around the world; it is also our historical heritage. Hebrew as a language was spoken and written over 3,000 years ago, making it the oldest language which is currently in use across the world. Not only that, but Hebrew is the original language of the Bible, which has been translated into hundreds of other languages and has been the "best seller of all-time"! Hebrew connects our past to the present and will accompany us on our journey to the future. It is vital to us as a People and carries great value in our culture. It is important that every student at Barrack learn Hebrew so s/he will be able to understand and appreciate the sacred texts in their original language as well as communicate with millions of people who speak Hebrew around the world today. It is invaluable to the maority of our students when they travel to study in Israel during their eleventh-grade semester at MUSS.

The Jack M. Barrack Hebrew Academy maintains an outstanding reputation for a successful program of Hebrew language learning. In the academic year 2010-2011, we are offering six different levels of Hebrew in the Middle School, nine levels in grades 9 and 10, and six in grades 11 and 12. In addition to the four language skills of understanding, speaking, reading, and writing- which are emphasized daily at each level- some of our top-level classes use curricular materials such as poems, dramas, essays and prose from Hebrew literature. Students in these classes can read complete books in Hebrew and are able to comment and analyse them critically. Students of this caliber are able to write papers of several pages in Hebrew.

In the NETA program, students view movies and listen to audio recordings of the various seifim (sections) found in the text. There is a great variety of songs as well as seifim referring to aspects of Jewish and Israeli culture, the Bible and Jewish legends as they relate to the modern themes. The hovrot (consumible books) number up to 25 beyond the 4 introductory booklets studied by beginners. This complete multi-year program provides a logical and comfortable way for students to learn the language of our past and present-day Israel.

Whether you are an absolute beginner or has studied Hebrew all your life, please know that the staff of the Hebrew department is a particularly caring one, concerned that every student succeed and advance to the next level with the appropriate skills. Help is readily available by any one of the teachers, providing assistance so students do not fall behind for any reason.


Department Files

  • Study Skills

 

All classes at the Hebrew Literature level are conducted completely in Hebrew. Our student will develop conversation skills and improve text reading (aloud) with comprehension.

 

The course work emphasizes independent reading and writing skills by developing dictionary skills for decoding (comprehension) and coding (enriching writing style).

 

Once a cycle we have discussions and oral presentations by students of current events, to build vocabulary in areas our literature material does not cover.

Students are expected to read three books per year in Hebrew, on their own (at home), write a 5 to 6 page book report (one report per trimester) and present an oral presentation of each book they read.

Students learn Hebrew word processing and are required to hand in long assignments and book reports using a computer and Hebrew language software.

Syllabus

Using the anthology as a source of reading material, we analyze the pieces using literary terminology and technique to determine what is said and how it is said. We distinguish spoken language from written narrative style. We discuss ideas and themes brought about by our reading. We compare similar themes presented by different writers or different pieces by the same writer. Periodically, there will be an extensive writing assignment and students will be asked to compare and contrast themes, writing style, characters and literary devices from one or more of the pieces that we studied.

Once a cycle we have discussions and oral presentations by students of current events, to build vocabulary in areas our literature material does not cover.

Students are required to read three books per year in Hebrew, on their own (at home), write a 5 to 6 page book report (one report per trimester) and present an oral presentation of each book they read.

You may use this outline for your book report assignment.

Assessment Policy

Oral and written assignments follow our study of each literary piece. Assessments usually require writing in-class compositions that will prove mastery of vocabulary and content of the piece we studied in class.

Final trimester grade will consist of:

50% tests and quizzes (a test is equal to two quizzes). Following each short unit, and sometimes at half point of a unit, we will have a quiz which includes new vocabulary mastery check, grammar and a written essay assignment. Tests will include several literary pieces and will require more independent reading and interpretation skills.

30% written assignments, in class or homework.

20% oral work, consists of oral participation in class, text reading (aloud), current events oral presentations and oral assignments like book report review.

In June, our students will write a final exam which will cover the material we studies during the whole trimester and will also include an un-seen piece which the students will have to read, comprehend by using decoding methods we studied over the year, and comment on by comparing/contrasting the new piece to material that we studied in class. The final exam will count for 10% of year's grade.
