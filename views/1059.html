Course Overview

Eleventh grade English focuses on the progression of American Literature.  This course’s first novel, The Scarlet Letter, is the perfect culmination of ideas studied during first trimester.  Later, we will study The Great Gatsby, Death of a Salesman, short stories, poetry, and several outside reading selections; at year’s end, students will submit a substantial original memoir.

 

Major Grades

Thesis papers; tests; memoir; short writing, at-home and in-class; vocabulary quizzes; reading quizzes, including multiple-choice assessments

 

Texts

Hawthorne, The Scarlet Letter; Fitzgerald, The Great Gatsby; Miller, Death of a Salesman;

Poetry photocopies; Short story photocopies; outside reading

 

Assessment Policy

All assignments will earn a numeric grade based on the weight of the assignment. For instance, a short homework assignment might be worth ten points, while a major thesis paper with multiple revisions might be worth over 100 points. At the end of each trimester, the average of all points earned will be divided by the total possible points to compute a student's grade. If a student earns, say, 270 out of a possible 300 points, the trimester grade would be 90% or A-minus.

Classroom Guidelines

Rules

  • Late Work: Points will be deducted for each day an assignment is late, including rough drafts.  I will deduct one-half letter grade for each non-Shabbat day the assignment is late.  Once a major assignment (paper, test) is late by one week, I will not accept it.  For homework assignments (worksheets, study questions, etc.), I will deduct one-half letter grade if it is one day late and I will not accept it after one day.
  • Self-Control: Disruption of class in any way will not be tolerated, especially talking when you are not supposed to be.
  • Cheating: Don’t plagiarize; JBHA policy is clear on the consequences.  I reserve the right to quiz you about the content of your paper or, if I find proof of plagiarism, fail you for that paper.  Remember, we have access to the same internet you do and it’s fairly obvious when your tutor is doing your work for you.  If you are unsure as to which websites are valuable for literary criticism and writing help, please see me or e-mail me.  If you are influenced by any ideas or direct quotes from any website, you must cite sources or risk a plagiarism charge.  By the way, SparkNotes is not helpful, as some students insist.  The “guides” are boring, uninspired, and strewn with errors.  Why bother? 
  • Excuses and Entitlement: I have a low tolerance for excuses.  Establish credibility.  Submit assignments and arrive to class on time, and if a genuine emergency arises, I will be more likely to act sympathetically.
  • Phones: Of course, I will confiscate a phone if I see one and bring it to Mrs. Farrell’s office after class.  If I suspect you are texting or using a phone surreptitiously, I will permanently move your seat to the front of the room or ask that you leave your phone on the front desk every day as you enter class.
  • Technology: Except by my special permission, you are not allowed to use iPads, computers, or any other technology in my class.  You need paper and pen/ pencil, and you are always encouraged to take notes in the books themselves.  All in-class assessments must also be hand-written, unless I decide to make an exception.  However, you will be required to submit all at-home writing by e-mail.  You must submit the work as a document, either Microsoft Word or Google Doc, AND you must paste your work directly into the text of your e-mail.

Section I: Fill-in-the-blank (5 minutes, 9 points)

Fill in each blank with the most appropriate name, term, or phrase from the above list.  Some names may appear more than once and some may not appear at all.

Sample 1: (Please write your answer on the sheets of paper provided) is the photographer who cannot keep his hands off the lever in the elevator.

Section II – Identification (10 minutes, 36 points)

 You must write 2-3 clear and concise sentences about the significance of any three of the following.  You must be as specific as possible in your explanations, and decide for yourself, as an educated reader, how best to address the “significance” of each.   

 

Sample 1: “They’re such beautiful shirts.”

Sample 2: “elegant young roughneck”

 

Section III – Quotes (25 minutes, 30 points)

You must write a short paragraph, with direct quotes, in response to any two of the following prompts.

 

Sample: His heart beat faster and faster as Daisy’s white face came up to his own. He knew that when he kissed this girl, and forever wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath, his mind would never romp again like the mind of God. Conduct a close reading of the above passage from the end of chapter six, using the following questions as your prompts.  Interpret the second sentence.  What does it reveal about Gatsby?  Analyze the third sentence: Explain how he is “listening . . . to the tuning-fork that had been struck upon a star.”  Why is it important that these sentences occur before the kiss?  Then, look at the last sentence:  In general, what happens to a flower after it reaches its fullest blossom?  How does this image of a blossoming flower help describe Gatsby’s ultimate failure to “win” Daisy?  You must write a paragraph (not bullet points) and quote directly from the passage when relevant.

Organization:

 

(ideas borrowed heavily and often quoted directly from the Writing Center at www.drew.edu)

 

Recommended: The point-by-point pattern:  When you use this structure, you work back and forth between the sources you consider in your paper discussing one point of similarity or difference at a time.  Each paragraph takes one feature or point of similarity or difference and discusses each source in relation to it.  With this method, you are holding the two books side-by-side, constantly analyzing each, in each paragraph.  This organizational pattern is standard in academic comparison and contrast essays in college.

 

Not bad, but my second choice: The continuous block pattern:  In this structure, you discuss first one item and then the other, in each section of your paper.  In other words, in each section, you would focus on one point of similarity or difference and compose one paragraph about one play and a second paragraph about the other play. 

 

Please try to avoid: The single block:  In this structure, you would entirely exhaust your points about one play before moving on to the second play, probably near the bottom of page two.  Usually, students like to wrap up this kind of paper by squeezing in one more body paragraph in which the comparison and contrast is made clear.  This method is sloppy and lacks a tight structure and natural flow.  It causes your reader to constantly flip back and forth to try to make the connections.  This method is a great way to start out, making notes for yourself, but you should bring your points together in one of the above patterns before you submit your paper to me.

EXTRA Literary Criticism

 

Harold Bloom on Death of a Salesman(from Bloom’s Literary Reference Online):

 

“Miller wants to give us a Willy Loman who is destroyed by social energies.  Fortunately for the continued dramatic validity of Death of a Salesman, something deeper than Miller’s political polemic pervades the play and makes it more than a parody of “the American Dream” of upward mobility, so that Willy Loman finally escapes the dubious fate of being a poor man’s Jay Gatsby.”

 

“Death of a Salesman is more the tragedy of a family than it is of an individual or of a society.”

 

“Miller [blends] Willy and the Lomans into a common grayness, so that they lack all color or exuberance and yield much of their pathos to a vision of social reductiveness, as if they were victims purely of the false dreams of their nation.”

 

“If Willy has tragic structure, it is because he is exiled from himself, and so can win no victory whatsoever.  We are terribly moved by Willy’s conviction that, if he is not successful, then he will not deserve to be loved by his family.  No possible success could assuage Willy’s tormented yearning to be popular, to be loved.”

 

“He does not die the death of a salesman.  He dies the death of a father, perhaps not the universal father, but a father central enough to touch the anguish of the universal.”

“What if I have nothing interesting to say?”

I feel really sorry for you.

 

(from What if? – a book for writers):  Who are you?  Somebody!

Here are suggestions for dislodging ideas about yourself:

·         List in detail all the places you have lived – one place per page.  This exercise will give you a sense of time and place.  You might even want to get very specific, say, by recounting all the kitchens, attics, bedrooms, etc.

·         Next, recall if you were happy or unhappy in those places.

·         Consider your parents’ relationship, from their point of view.

·         List important family members.  What are the dynamics of your nuclear family?  Extended family?  Leave space for unfinished family business.

·         List smells – indoor and outdoor – and the memories they conjure up.

·         Do you have any recurring dreams or nightmares?

·         Ask yourself, what did I care about when I was five?  Ten?  Fifteen?  Last trimester?  Last month?  What do I care about now?

·         What is your five-year plan?  Ten-year plan?

 

--from Anne Bernays and Pamela Painter, What if? Writing Exercises for Fiction Writers.  New York:

                 Longman, 1995.  Page 25.
